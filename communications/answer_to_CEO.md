## 1. Strategic Scope: Option 3 (Both)
To answer your core question directly: **We are executing Option 3.**

We will test **New Materials** (specifically Binder Chemistries) AND **New Ratios** (Metal/Carbon balance) simultaneously.

**Justification:**
Swelling in MG1157 is a function of two coupled factors:
1.  **The Matrix Chemistry (The "Material"):** This defines *how much* water the structure wants to absorb (Thermodynamic Potential).
2.  **The Structural Density (The "Ratio"):** This defines *how much space* is available for that expansion to happen before it locks against the holder (Mechanical Constraint).

If we only test ratios, we are just band-aiding the existing chemistry. If we only test materials at 50% Ag, we might miss a high-performance, low-cost solution at 30% Ag. Given our "Unlimited Funds" directive, limiting the scope would be negligent. We will manage the complexity via high-throughput screening (Phase 2).

## 2. A Critical Addition: The "Carbon Phase" (My Recommendation)
While we are aggressive on Binders and Metals, I must express a strong professional interest in revisiting the **Carbon Phase** itself.

Currently, we treat "Graphite" as a generic commodity. However, the difference between **Natural Flake Graphite**, **Synthetic Electro-Graphite**, and **Amorphous Coke** is profound regarding lattice intercalation (how water gets inside the crystal structure).

**Proposal:** I want to introduce a "Carbon Source" variable into the matrix.
*   *Hypothesis:* Synthetic graphite, having a more ordered structure but fewer edge defects than natural flake, might resist moisture intercalation better, even with the same binder.
*   *Impact:* If we find a hydrophobic carbon source, we might solve the problem at the root, reducing reliance on expensive silver or complex additives.
